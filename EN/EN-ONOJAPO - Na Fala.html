IN SPEECH

Mimetic words or onomatopoeia are an essential part of everyday communication in Japan. While in many languages the use of these terms is limited or restricted to children's contexts, in Japanese they are legitimate expressive tools in almost any situation: they appear in informal conversations, TV shows, magazine articles, advertising, and even professional speeches. Japanese uses these words to convey nuances that would be difficult to express with conventional adjectives or verbs alone, allowing the speaker to describe textures, movements, sensations, emotions, and atmospheres in an immediate and vivid way. For those learning the language, mastering these words is an important step toward sounding natural and fully understanding how Japanese people perceive the world.

One of the most interesting features of mimetic words in Japanese speech is the possibility of combining them with the verb する (suru) — the verb “to do.” This construction transforms the mimetic word into an action or state verb, allowing it to function as a complete predicate. This increases the flexibility of the language, as emotions, rhythms, and sensations can become grammatical actions. For example, にこにこする (nikoniko suru) means “to smile cheerfully,” わくわくする (wakuwaku suru) expresses “to be excited,” and どきどきする (dokidoki suru) indicates that the heart is “beating fast,” whether due to anxiety, excitement, or fear. In practice, this pattern is extremely common and appears in everything from everyday conversations to dialogues in manga, soap operas, and variety shows.

Japanese mimetic words are traditionally divided into five categories, each representing a specific type of phenomenon. These categories help us understand how the language organizes sensory and psychological experiences:

Giseigo 擬声語 — words that imitate sounds produced by living beings, such as animals and people. E.g.: にゃー (nyaa – meow), わんわん (wanwan – bark).

Giongo 擬音語 — words that imitate natural sounds or inanimate objects. Ex.: ざあざあ (zaazaa – heavy rain), ごろごろ (gorogoro – thunder).

Gitaigo 擬態語 — words that describe states, conditions, or movements without actual sound. Examples: きらきら (kirakira – sparkling), ふわふわ (fuwafuwa – soft, fluffy).

Gijougo 擬情語 — words that express internal feelings or emotions. Ex.: いらいら (iraira – irritated), びくびく (bikubiku – scared).

Giyougo 擬容語 — words that describe modes of action performed by people or animate beings. Ex.: もたもた (motamota – to act slowly), きびきび (kibikibi – to act energetically).
